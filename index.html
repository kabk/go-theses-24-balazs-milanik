  • Abstract

    I was around 3 years old in 2004, when my parents finally decided that I was old enough to bring me along to the capital of Hungary, Budapest. Other than falling heads first into a fountain in the Buda castle, the most memorable moments of my visit came from the discovery of the metro under the city. I was amazed by this intricate, yet robust underground system and all of its audio-visual aspects: the colourful and grandiose interiors of the stations, the smell of electricity, the sounds of announcements and loud rumbling of the rolling stocks made, or the seemingly never-ending escalators that connected these cut-off spaces with the surface above.

    Since some 19 years after my first encounters with this mode of transportation not only did I grew older, but so did the environment that I have discovered at the time. The Budapest metro has gone through a tumultuous and constant development of constructions and reconstructions that originally started way back in 1896 with the opening of continental Europe’s first metro line and as of right now ended with the latest construction works being finished in 2023.

    Having started to study in the field of graphic design, I firmly believe that our craft has a lot to do with how these environments are conveyed. Metros constitute a part in a city which is fully designed from the length of the tracks all the way to the typefaces used on signage. It is a product of human origin.

    Thus, I would like to focus on the exploration of metro line three, which other than being the busiest and longest line of the city also has gone through a recent renovation process that was aimed at refurbishing the most run-down line in the city.

     


  • Of Metro Line Three In Brief

    The észak–déli metróvonal (north-south metro line) commonly referred to as the kék metró (blue metro) is the longest and busiest line in the Budapest public transport system. Counting 20 stations on a 16,5km long spread on the Pest (east) side of the city, the line serves as a connection between commercial industrial and residential areas, while also accessing important public points such as hospitals, bus terminals, train stations, or other metro lines. With a daily ridership of 500000, it has become an organic part of the Budapest metropolitan area.

    It was initially planned and built in the socialist era between 1970–1990 that played an important role in its audio-visual conditions. In my opinion, contemporary designers’ architects, and planners have envisioned often experimental, and in some cases straight-up artistic graphical plans for the stations, cars, signs, and sounds, informed by the taste of the time elements of the 70s and 80s.

    Despite my personal taste in funky socialist designs, that I might also appreciate more since I never had to use the system daily, in the eyes of passengers the metro was rather a grim environment of another time. If one had taken line three in the past decades on a regular basis, their experiences mostly have consisted of listening to the loud rumbling of old Soviet trains that sped in-between dirty and poorly lit stations of old-fashioned ran-down designs, commanded by melancholic announcements, checked and supervised by shady inspectors, led by confusing signage, all this below the ground.

    While the line overall remained operational, over the years it seemed like that time had stopped under the ground, while on the surface it sped away, leaving the spaces as hidden stains on the capitals ever evolving image. As decades passed, many layers of junk have started to stack up both in the visual and functional sense. In simple terms: the metro became outdated.
    The steady deterioration of the line was due to the lack of financial and systemic maintenance. Almost all elements from signage and ornaments to the metro cars and tracks were basically left to rot to an extent that by the end of 2010s, even the safety of passengers using the metro in general became a realistic concern. After a recent and long needed renovation of line three beginning on the 4th of November, 2017, and the complete change of the metro cars in early 2018, line three is now just as new as it was in it’s initial opening.

    As the ideas of rapid movement that emerge both as a need and a result of Western metropolitan life constitute a need the overarches socialist and capitalist ideologies, the way in which the metro functions remains unchanged. In this sense the line has received more of a makeover, rather than a new purpose/function, which as we will see, creates an interesting scale on which the spaces of line three either feel completely new while in others the differences between the new and old lines are quite hard to spot.With that, I would like to commence our travel with our descent to the first stage: the underpass.
